<p> <a href="https://writing.jasonheppler.org/2024/12/27/attempting-a-new-sourdough-recipe/" class="microblog_permalink">2024-12-27</a> </p> <hr> <b>Year of Foundations</b> 
<p> </p> 
<p>I’m not a regular year-end blogger other than yearly theme posts but I wanted to take a moment at the end of 2024 to reflect amidst my own 2025 theme. One thing I’ve done pretty consistently since 2021 is to consider a yearly theme. Those have been:</p> <ul> <li> <a href="https://social.jasonheppler.org/2022/12/19/yearly-theme.html">2022</a>: Year of Lagom</li> <li> <a href="https://social.jasonheppler.org/2022/12/19/yearly-theme.html">2023</a>: Year of Festina Lente</li> <li> <a href="https://jasonheppler.org/2023/01/26/yearly-theme-2023/">2024</a>: Year of Work</li> <li>2025: <strong>Year of Foundation</strong> </li> </ul> 
<p><strong>Foundation</strong> for me means a few things: new structures in place around health and fitness, some long-term work we’ll be doing around our acreage (barn restoration, garden expansion, orchard expansion, prairie re-wilding), career goals, and some long-term household tasks. So this year will be spent, in part, with an eye towards the future. We’ve also had, in some ways, a tough 2024. This year will serve as something of a reset as well.</p> 
<p>If you are interested in what I’ve been reading, you can check out <a href="https://social.jasonheppler.org/library/">my micro.blog reading log</a>.</p> 
<p><img src="https://writing.jasonheppler.org/uploads/2025/christmas-2024.png" alt="Auto-generated description: A weathered red wooden door features a green wreath with a red bow at its center." style="max-width: 100%; height: auto;"></p> <h3> <a href="#new-book-new-digital-history-next-book-new-blogging" aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" id="new-book-new-digital-history-next-book-new-blogging"></a>New Book, New Digital History, Next Book, New Blogging</h3> 
<p>In March 2024, my book <em>Silicon Valley and the Environmental Inequalities of High Tech Urbanism</em> came out with the University of Oklahoma Press. I’m really proud of how well the book turned out and it’s been well-received (including my neighbor, who unbeknownst to me told me he was reading it and really liked it. It’s the best review I could’ve gotten). As part of the book’s release, I did a <a href="https://newnatures.org/greenhouse/events/booktalk/online-book-talk-heppler-silicon-valley-and-the-environmental-inequalities/">couple</a> of <a href="https://draftingthepast.com/podcast-episodes/episode-45-jason-heppler-wants-tools-that-fit-his-questions/">podcasts</a> and visited a few classes to talk about the project. All in all, I’m happy with the work.</p> 
<p>It was also a busy time around RRCHNM. We launched several new projects this year, including:</p> <ul> <li>The <a href="https://jasonheppler.org/2024/05/21/django-dev/">Arnham Postal History project</a>, providing a database and mapping environment for tracing postal routes in the Netherlands during World War II.<sup class="footnote-ref"><a href="#fn-1" id="fnref-1" data-footnote-ref>1</a></sup> </li> <li> <em>La Sfera, or The Globe</em> by Gregorio Dati: Still in development but soft launched a couple of months ago, the project is a critical edition of Gregorio Dati’s <em>La sfera</em> manuscript written sometime around 1430.</li> <li> <a href="%5Bhttps://denigmanuscript.org%5D(https://denigmanuscript.org/)">The Denig Manuscript</a>: In collaboration with the Winterthur Museum, Library and Gardens, we provide an interface to read, view, and hear the eighteenth-century illuminated manuscript by the Pennsylvania Dutch merchant Ludwig Denig.</li> <li> <a href="%5Bhttps://connectingthreads.co.uk%5D(https://connectingthreads.co.uk/)">Connecting Threads</a>: Launched in October at an event in London, Connecting Threads seeks to investigate the consumption of Indian and Indian-imitation fabrics by communities of the global south. Much more is coming in the next year. To pursue that work, Deepthi Murali and I <a href="https://rrchnm.org/news/deepthi-murali-and-jason-heppler-receive-neh-funding-for-connecting-threads-project/">won an NEH grant</a> to continue the work.</li> <li>Lots of progress on existing projects as well: a <a href="%5Bhttps://deathbynumbers.org%5D(https://deathbynumbers.org/)">redesign of the Bills of Mortality website</a>; new content for <a href="%5Bhttps://religiousecologies.org%5D(https://religiousecologies.org/)">Religious Ecologies</a>; new work for the <a href="https://www.neh.gov/news/kelly-receives-funding-civil-war-graffiti-preservation">Civil War Graffiti project</a>.</li> <li>I’ll be teaching Digital Public History in our Digital Public Humanities certificate program again this spring.</li> <li>I also began a new mentorship with one of our graduate students helping me out with development work. It’s been a lot of fun and, I hope, engaging and useful for her.</li> </ul> 
<p>I’ve begun work on my next book as well this year. I’m returning to some work I completed in graduate school on the Sagebrush Rebellion in the northern plains. I’m hoping to spend a lot of time in the archives this year—in Colorado, Wyoming, Montana, South Dakota, North Dakota, Nebraska—so I can make some further progress on the work. Relatedly, I’ve started a side blog/newsletter called <a href="https://ink.jasonheppler.org">Tack &amp; Ink</a> where I’ll be writing about my research in progress. I hope you’ll consider subscribing.</p> <h3> <a href="#life" aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" id="life"></a>Life</h3>
