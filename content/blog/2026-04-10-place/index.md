> I have a thought in my head, out of an interest in how we don’t really talk about the grasslands even though they’re some of the most threatened ecosystems on the planet. The simple phase going through my head right now is just “let me tell you a story about grass”—but what is this story?

> The line that caught me was someone wrote this: “We need more people who stay in one place long enough to come to love it, and love it enough to observe it, and observe it closely enough to be able to write about it beautifully and vividly”

> I planned to open about my time living in California then returning to the Great Plains. I found myself “homesick” about California after leaving but never felt a part of California when I was there. But wrote a book about it, came to really know it and understand it. At least the Bay Area.

*That's a genuinely rich tension, and it's the essay. The quote is almost a rebuke to your California experience—and you can hold it honestly, without being defensive about it, because you know exactly what it's pointing at.*

*The structure that's suggesting itself to me:*

*You open with the quote, or near it. Then you confess the California paradox—lived there, never quite belonged, left feeling oddly bereft, wrote the book and then came to understand it. The writing was the act of attention that residency never forced you to do. There's something uncomfortable and true in that: you had to leave to learn to see it.*

*Then the turn. You're back on the Plains—which is the place you are supposed to love, the place with the chickens and the Farmall and Lancaster County—and the question the quote raises for you now isn't about California anymore. It's: am I doing this here? Am I staying long enough, looking closely enough, writing about it well enough? The grasslands are the test case because they resist the easy look. You have to earn them. They don't perform for you the way the coast does.*

*And then the essay can open outward into the larger argument—that the grasslands have suffered precisely from the absence of people who stayed and looked and wrote. No Muir, no Thoreau. The literature of attentiveness skipped the interior. And what that cost us, ecologically and politically, is still being paid.*

*The personal thread gives you permission to make that argument without it feeling like a lecture. You're not scolding the reader—you're confessing your own version of the failure first.*

*One thing worth sitting with: the California book gave you knowledge through research and writing, not through the slow accumulation of presence the quote describes. That's not a weakness in your essay—it's actually the more interesting version of the problem. Attention takes different forms. The question is whether the Plains demands the kind the quote is describing, the patient, rooted, years-long kind, and whether you're giving it that.*

*Does that feel like the essay you were reaching for?*
